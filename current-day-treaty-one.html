Treaty One

Unfulfilled Promises

After the signing of Treaty One, many outside promises on the side of the Canadian government were not fulfilled. No provisions were included for agricultural implements, clothing and animals that were promised. Many Indigenous People voiced their concerns about the unfulfilled promises in February 1872, yet the issues were not resolved by the federal government until April 30th, 1975.

Broken Promises: Hunting and Fishing Rights

The negotiators from the side of the Canadian government failed to include a provision on hunting and fishing rights in the written text of Treaty One, although they verbally promised to Indigenous Peoples that they would retain such rights in the ceded territory. As the years went on, the settler government of Manitoba began restricting access to Indigenous Peoples to game and fishing, which violated these promises.


"...Canada is a test case for a grand notion: the notion that dissimilar peoples can share lands, resources, power, and dreams while respecting and sustaining their differences. The story of Canada is the story of many such peoples, trying and failing and trying again, to live together in peace and harmony. But there cannot be peace or harmony unless there is justice..."


- Chief Georges Erasmus and Justice René Dussault, The Royal Commission on Aboriginal Peoples


Implementation of Treaty One Today

Treaty One established clearly defined land ownership and jurisdiction, and foster a new relationship based on mutual respect, trust and understanding. In addition, it provides guidelines on how Europeans can use the traditional land that Indigenous Peoples have occupied. They also provide documents with Indigenous rights as written in the treaties. Although there are many instances of conflict due to the unfulfilled promises historically, today Treaty One is still implemented in many ways. One of those ways is by building reserves, most notably the plans for a Naawi-Oodena urban reserve, also known as Former Kapyong Barracks Master Plan.

The Naawi-Oodena Master Plan

The Naawi-Oodena Master Plan oversees the development of former Kapyong Barracks into an urban reserve for Treaty One First Nations, Winnipeg, Manitoba and Canada for future generations. The development of the lands will help shape Winnipeg’s future urban growth, generate significant economic investment and connect communities. The comprehensive planning and framework of the Master Plan wants to transform the 160-acres site into a diverse mixed-use community, emphasizing Indigenous culture, design-excellence and connectivity with the surrounding neighborhoods.



Aspirations for the Master Plan

There are seven main aspirations for the Master Plan. Those include:

  1. To generate success and cultivate new partnerships
  2. Make a welcoming and inclusive community
  3. Celebrate the identity and culture of First Nations
  4. Promote sustainable urban development
  5. Progress new expression in Indigenous design
  6. Share Indigenous knowledge and teachings
  7. Display innovation in design and urban development
